## Question 1: Compare and Contrast Digital Evidence and Non-Digital Evidence

### Similarities

**Admissibility in Court**
All evidence, regardless of digital or physical nature, must adhere to proper legal standards to ensure admissibility in a court of law. For evidence to be admissible it must be authentic, relevant, and not violate rules of evidence.

**Evidence Collection Procedures**
All evidence must be preserved carefully and maintained with integrity to be admissible in court. Evidence that has been tampered with could be inadmissible and thrown out during a court case due to tampering or breaking the chain of custody procedures.

**Analysis of the Evidentiary Information**
Regardless of type, both digital and non-digital evidence are examined rigorously by forensic experts. Experts must extract and document data carefully in order to analyze and draw conclusions about which parts are relevant to the investigation or court proceeding.

### Differences

**Nature of Evidence**
Digital evidence derives from a digital format such as emails, computer or cloud files, and social media posts. Non-digital evidence involves a physical component or object — DNA, fingerprints, weapons, and substances are among the most common.

**Vulnerability and Volatility**
Digital evidence is typically collected with specialized software and/or hardware. Non-digital evidence is usually obtained through collection kits, physically taking items into the chain of custody, and photography. Non-digital evidence tends to be less susceptible to alteration once secured by authorities. Digital evidence, on the other hand, can be easily destroyed or altered both physically and remotely.

**Analytical Complexity**
Non-digital evidence tends to be easier to decipher via visual inspection, fingerprint or DNA analysis, and chemical testing. Digital evidence usually entails complex data recovery processes such as decryption, and specialized software may be required to interpret certain data. Documentation is even more critical for digital evidence as it could easily be altered or deleted either by honest mistake or for nefarious reasons.

### Scenario: Non-Digital Evidence Used to Refute Digital Evidence

A Chase credit card customer is attempting to refute fraudulent charges. Digitally, the investigation found that the correct username and password were used, the customer's known device with two-factor authentication was the source, and the customer's home IP address matched. From Chase's standpoint, the purchases appeared legitimate.

However, non-digital evidence refuted this conclusion. The customer presented hotel receipts proving no one was home during the time of the purchases. Delivery receipts showed the customer's name but a mismatched signature. Photos, other credit card records, and home security camera footage with timestamps confirmed the entire family was away — and showed a stranger picking up the packages.

Though digital evidence initially appeared to confirm guilt, further investigation and non-digital evidence proved the transactions were fraudulent.

## Question 2: Search Warrants and Digital Evidence

A search warrant is a legal mechanism that authorities in the United States must obtain to lawfully search someone's property or personal information. Due to the Fourth Amendment of the U.S. Constitution, authorities must demonstrate probable cause to believe evidence of a crime exists in the location they are asking to search. A detailed affidavit must be presented to a judge, who decides whether the inquiry has merit.

Each warrant must be specific in what evidence is being sought — this is known as the **scope of a warrant**, put in place to minimize unnecessary search and seizure and protect citizens' right to privacy.

### The Warrant Process

1. **Probable Cause** — The investigator prepares an affidavit detailing the facts supporting probable cause and what evidence they hope to obtain.
2. **Judicial Review** — The affidavit is submitted to a judge or magistrate to review.
3. **Execution** — If granted, the search and seizure must comply with the scope of the warrant or risk evidence being thrown out.

### Digital Evidence Considerations

Digital evidence warrants are more complex. Courts may view non-specific affidavits as overbroad. Authorities must explicitly detail what digital data they are seeking. Given that digital files can be disguised or encrypted, digital warrants often do not include a search protocol since it would be extremely difficult to limit the scope to specific directories or file types.

Judges may add stipulations such as timing rules. Under North Carolina law G.S. 15A-248, a search warrant must be executed within 48 hours of issuance. If a secondary crime is discovered during execution, a second warrant should be obtained to ensure admissibility. Documentation at every stage is critical.

## Question 3: The Plain View Doctrine Applied to Digital Evidence

The Plain View Doctrine allows law enforcement to seize evidence that is immediately visible or accessible during a lawful encounter — without the need for a warrant.

**Scenario:** In an apartment complex, a neighbor calls 911 over a peeping-tom complaint. When an officer arrives and Neighbor D opens the door, the officer sees a computer in plain view displaying what appears to be child pornography. The officer can lawfully detain the individual and begin securing the evidence.

However, due to the evolving and often unclear legal standards around digital evidence, it is best practice for the officer to detain the suspect, preserve the evidence, and seek a proper warrant. A defense attorney could argue the computer contents were not "in plain view" and that the officer had no lawful basis to search the device — making any findings potentially inadmissible.

## Question 4: Exigent Circumstances Applied to Digital Evidence

The Exigent Circumstances Doctrine allows government officials — police, EMTs, firefighters — to set aside Fourth Amendment rights without a warrant when there is an emergent and immediate need.

> *"Circumstances that would cause a reasonable person to believe that entry or other relevant prompt action was necessary to prevent physical harm to officers or other persons, the destruction of relevant evidence, the escape of a suspect, or some other consequence improperly frustrating legitimate law enforcement efforts."* — SADRP Penal Code

**Scenario:** Investigators are monitoring a known hacker who is actively attempting to remotely wipe a precinct's servers, disrupting communications with officers and emergency personnel while destroying digital evidence. There is an immediate threat to life and safety, as well as imminent destruction of evidence. A cybersecurity investigator remotely accesses the hacker's system to stop the attack and preserve evidence.

A defense attorney might challenge the admissibility of evidence by arguing a privacy violation — particularly if the attack was launched from a public space such as a library, where identifying the actual user would require additional corroborating evidence such as security footage or cell phone location data.
